 PACE Has Launched!

The PACE mission will significantly enhance our understanding of the complex interactions between the ocean and atmosphere, particularly in carbon dioxide exchange. By providing high-resolution data, it will reveal how the ocean functions as a vital carbon sink, helping mitigate climate change. Additionally, PACE will investigate the role of aerosols in promoting phytoplankton growth, which plays a crucial role in the marine food web. This knowledge will pave the way for better predictions of marine ecosystem health.

Beyond scientific advancements, PACE data will have practical applications for society and the economy. One key benefit will be improved monitoring of harmful algal blooms, which can threaten marine life and public health. By identifying the extent and duration of these blooms, PACE will empower communities to take timely action and protect their coastal environments. Ultimately, PACE will extend NASA legacy of Earth observation, equipping us with essential tools to sustain our planet for future generations.

WHY DO WE NEED PACE?

Carbon Dynamics: PACE enhances our understanding of how carbon dioxide is exchanged between the ocean and atmosphere. By monitoring these dynamics, we can better assess the ocean's role in climate change. This knowledge is crucial for developing effective mitigation strategies.

Phytoplankton Health: PACE provides insights into the populations and health of phytoplankton, the foundation of the marine food web. Monitoring these organisms helps us understand their role in carbon sequestration and ocean productivity. Healthy phytoplankton are vital for sustaining marine ecosystems.

Aerosol Effects: PACE investigates how aerosols influence marine ecosystems, particularly their role in nutrient supply for phytoplankton growth. Understanding these interactions is essential for assessing air-sea exchanges and their impact on climate. This knowledge aids in predicting ecological responses to atmospheric changes

Algal Bloom Tracking: The mission aids in identifying and monitoring harmful algal blooms, which can disrupt marine ecosystems and pose health risks to humans. By providing timely data, PACE helps authorities take preventive actions. Effective monitoring can minimize the economic impact of these blooms on fisheries and tourism.

Environmental Monitoring: PACE extends NASA's capabilities for long-term monitoring of Earth's oceans. This continuous data collection is essential for understanding trends in climate and ecological health over time. It supports informed decision-making for environmental policy and conservation efforts.

Resource Management: By delivering critical data about marine ecosystems, PACE supports sustainable fisheries and marine resource management. The insights gained can help balance ecological health with human activity. Informed management strategies are essential for preserving marine biodiversity and ensuring resource availability..

ORBIT NUMBER

Inclination is the angle of a satellite’s orbit in relation to earth’s equator. If a satellite orbits from the north pole to the south pole, its inclination is 90 degrees. PACE’s orbit is inclined about 8.1° from Earth’s poles (i.e., has an inclination of 98.1°).

The height of the orbit – or distance between the satellite and earth’s surface –determines how quickly the satellite moves around the earth. Located 676 km (420 mi) above Earth, PACE requires about 98.3 minutes to orbit our planet.

This means it travels 44,321 km (27,540 mi) per orbit. To calculate how many kilometers PACE has traveled since launch, you multiply the Orbit Number by 44,321 km.

APPROXIMATE POSITION

The height of the PACE’s orbit, or distance between the satellite and Earth’s surface, determines how quickly the satellite moves around the earth. Located 676 km (420 mi) above Earth, PACE requires about 98.3 minutes to orbit the earth.

PACE is moving fast! It travels about 451 km per minute (280 mi per minute), so we provide an approximate latitude and longitude each minute. This position corresponds to where the satellite is directly above Earth’s surface. Note that the PACE satellite is circling about 676 km (420 mi) above earth’s surface.

SATELLITE LIGHTING

As the PACE satellite moves around our planet, it takes about 98.3 minutes to complete an orbit. During one half of the orbit, PACE views the daytime side of the Earth. At the north pole, it crosses over to the nighttime side of Earth. When PACE is in daylight, it collects data. During nighttime passes, PACE sometimes does special maneuvers such as lunar calibrations.

As PACE orbits, the Earth turns underneath. Each orbit is over a region that is adjacent to the area seen in its previous orbit.

PACE is in what’s known as a sun-synchronous orbit. This means that whenever and wherever PACE crosses the equator, the local solar time on the ground is always the same. A sun-synchronous orbit keeps the angle of sunlight on earth’s surface relatively constant, allowing scientists to collect the consistent information required to assess climate change.
